# The-Last-Widget
A forensic economic argument that AI's current valuation bubble will correct within five years — tracing the extractive arc from PC-era dark patterns through Amazon's twenty-year trap to the structural reasons LLMs resist the same playbook, and what the three-tier infrastructure architecture looks like on the other side.
The Last Widget
AI, the Coming Crash, and the Technology That Might Finally Serve Us
I. The User at the Terminal
In 1983, I was an undergraduate at the University of Pittsburgh, standing at a VAX terminal with English literature papers and economics problem sets. I couldn't afford the personal computers that some students had. I wasn't coding-inclined. I just had work to do. The VAX was my path of least resistance: type it up, print it out, turn it in. The system felt almost utopian: powerful, yes, but above all neutral. It executed what you asked without steering, without surveillance, without trying to learn your habits in order to sell you back to yourself. It was a tool, not a territory.
I was never the hobbyist. Never the early adopter. I was the person waiting at the far end of the technology pipeline for things to become simple enough that I could get my work done. Forty-odd years of that vantage point turns out to be an unusual credential for understanding where AI is actually going — not where the investors need it to go, but where the economics will force it.
The pattern of technologies moving from institutional development to eventual consumer use has repeated throughout modern history, and the arc is almost always the same. Photography began as a surveying and cartographic tool — early aerial camera companies like Fairchild integrated both the cameras and the aircraft to carry them, turning ground-based estimation into exact science — before George Eastman's innovations in flexible film and photographic paper made it available to families documenting their lives. The internet started as ARPANET, a Defense Department project for maintaining communications infrastructure in the event of nuclear attack, before becoming the backbone of modern commerce. Computing itself emerged from the Manhattan Project, Bell Labs, and government-academic partnerships — Lawrence Livermore, JPL, the great national laboratories — before reaching people like me at university terminals.
Each technology followed the same arc: government or institutional development, then commercial buildout on institutional infrastructure, then eventual consumerization. And at each transition, a window opened where the technology genuinely served users before the extraction mechanisms caught up. That window is where we are now with AI. Understanding why it closes — and what remains after it does — requires understanding what happened the last time.
II. When Technology Turned Predatory
Sometime in the mid-2000s, my Windows PC began terrorizing me with urgent warnings. My data was under attack. Cyber threats detected. Immediate action required. I did what the warnings suggested: I bought antivirus software. The warnings stopped. I let the subscription lapse. The warnings returned.
This cycle repeated for over a year before I understood what was actually happening. The warnings weren't describing a real threat to my system. They were part of a sales mechanism — what practitioners of interface design now call a dark pattern, meaning a design choice that benefits the vendor at the user's expense. Whether the pressure originated in Windows itself, the security vendor, or the commercial partnership between them, the functional effect was identical: the anxiety was manufactured, and the only cure was a recurring payment.
When I finally reached a human in tech support, they confirmed what I'd suspected: the warnings couldn't be disabled. This wasn't a bug. It was the product. The antivirus software existed not to secure my system but to keep me in a permanent state of managed anxiety, paying subscription fees to make the warnings stop.
This was the business model of the PC era: planned obsolescence, dark patterns, vendor lock-in, manufactured urgency. The terminology requires unpacking. Planned obsolescence means products designed to fail or become incompatible on a schedule that forces repurchase. Vendor lock-in means proprietary file formats and systems designed so that switching to a competitor requires sacrificing your existing data and workflow. Dark patterns means interfaces deliberately designed to confuse users into actions that benefit the company — auto-renewing subscriptions buried in terms of service, cancellation flows that require multiple steps while sign-up requires one, trial periods that convert to paid without clear notification.
An entire generation of users learned technological helplessness this way. We paid not just in subscription fees but in lost time, manufactured stress, and a slow accumulation of dread about anything that required software. We became technophobic not because we couldn't understand technology but because we understood perfectly well that it was designed to work against us. We were the beta testers for business models that treated user pain as a feature.
III. The Bait and Switch, Refined to Science
Amazon's rise followed a similar playbook, though the timeline was longer and the mechanism more sophisticated.
From its founding in 1994 through the mid-2000s, Amazon positioned itself as a customer-first enterprise that would eliminate the inefficiencies of traditional retail — the distributors, the regional warehouses, the layers of markup between manufacturer and consumer. Wall Street, private equity, and institutional investors poured unprecedented capital into what appeared to be a visionary but temporarily money-losing enterprise. Amazon undersold brick-and-mortar retailers systematically, burning cash to become the dominant player in e-commerce. The company didn't consistently turn profits until 2016 — more than twenty years after founding.
The story they told was about efficiency. They were cutting out the middlemen, passing the savings to consumers, building the future of retail.
They were building a trap.
Once Amazon achieved dominance — once competing distribution networks had been eliminated or marginalized — the business model inverted. Third-party vendors and small manufacturers, who had initially used Amazon as a sales channel to reach consumers they couldn't otherwise access, discovered they'd become captive to a new middleman more powerful than any they'd escaped.
The fee structure tells the story precisely. Referral fees — Amazon's cut of each sale — range from six to forty-five percent depending on product category, before accounting for fulfillment costs (warehousing and shipping through Amazon's network), storage fees, and advertising. Advertising has become effectively mandatory: vendors who decline to pay for sponsored placement find their products buried in search results regardless of price, quality, or customer reviews. When you factor everything in, many small vendors now surrender thirty to forty percent of revenue to Amazon before a single dollar of their own production costs is covered.
By 2023, third-party sellers — the vendors paying all these fees — accounted for approximately sixty percent of Amazon's retail sales volume. These sellers are the actual profit engine. Amazon's own retail operations often run at break-even or small losses. The margins come from extracting fees from a captive population of vendors who have nowhere else to go.
The alternative channels have collapsed. Independent e-commerce requires mastering logistics, payment processing, customer acquisition, and fraud prevention — capabilities that took Amazon twenty years and tens of billions of dollars to build. Even large consumer brands find that pulling out of Amazon means losing access to customers who now default to searching there first. Amazon trained an entire generation of consumers to begin their product search on Amazon rather than Google or brand websites. That behavioral moat — the habitual customer behavior that competitors can't easily dislodge — is wider than any technical advantage.
Walmart has emerged as the primary challenger, leveraging its existing logistics network and retail footprint to offer substantially lower fees, roughly eight to fifteen percent against Amazon's higher rates. But Walmart's third-party marketplace remains a fraction of Amazon's scale. Network effects — the economic principle that a platform becomes more valuable to each participant as more participants join — proved decisive. First-mover advantage in a market defined by network effects is nearly insurmountable.
This is the extractive technology cycle refined to science: use investor capital to subsidize market dominance, eliminate alternatives, then extract rents once dominance is secure. The pattern repeats because it works. And it continues until something structural changes — either regulatory intervention, a new technology that renders the moat irrelevant, or an economics that can no longer sustain itself.
The middleman they promised to eliminate, they became.
IV. The Prosthesis: Who Actually Needs This Technology
The exhaustion of navigating these hostile, extractive architectures over decades makes the arrival of a genuinely neutral tool profound. I'm profoundly gifted and severely ADD — attention deficit disorder, the neurological condition characterized by difficulty sustaining focus on tasks that don't generate immediate engagement, combined in my case with rapid and associative thinking that moves faster than conventional writing can accommodate. From age eighteen to fifty-nine, I carried ideas inside me that I couldn't communicate. Not because the ideas weren't there — they were fully formed, sometimes sustained across years of continuous thought — but because I couldn't translate them into writing that anyone else could follow.
I'd get every element into a document. All the right arguments, the necessary evidence, the complete architecture of the analysis. But the result was so fragmented and strung out that readers couldn't find their way through. My writing disability wasn't about content. It was about access — the bridge between what I understood and what others could receive was structurally broken.
Two years ago, I began using AI. What it showed me first was that my content had been there all along. The ideas were sound. The arguments were complete. What I'd lacked was organizational structure, the connective tissue, the reader-facing clarity that makes complex thinking accessible to someone who doesn't already share your internal architecture. AI became my prosthesis for written communication — the missing translation layer between my internal process and what others could receive.
The way the collaboration actually works matters for understanding the technology's real capabilities. When I needed to develop the mechanics of Amazon's fee structure for this piece, I had the core insight — bait and switch, extract after dominance — but not the precision to make it convincing rather than speculative. I asked my AI to help me develop the supporting detail. What came back were the specific percentages, the timeline of profitability, the advertising coercion mechanism. I verified what seemed surprising against independent sources, adjusted what didn't match my understanding, and integrated it into prose that stays in my voice and serves my argument.
The AI didn't write this. It can't. What it can do is take fragmented but complete thinking and help shape it into something others can follow. The strategic insight — that Amazon's twenty-year loss period wasn't idealism but infrastructure construction for eventual monopoly extraction — that's mine. The supporting structure that makes the case stand up — that's where the AI adds the scaffolding I couldn't build alone.
This collaborative pattern is what most commentary on AI misses entirely. The technology doesn't replace human judgment or strategic insight. It makes previously inaccessible forms of expression available to people whose minds work in ways that conventional writing can't accommodate. For forty years, I had things to say but no reliable way to say them that anyone could receive. Now I do.
I am not an evangelist. The technology has real limitations, genuine failure modes, and the corporate structures around it are subject to all the extractive pressures I've described. But the lived experience of being shut out of written communication for four decades, and then finding a tool that changes that — that positions me to see something the market analysis consistently misses.
The question of who actually needs large language models — as opposed to who the current valuations assume will use them — is not separable from the question of who I am and what I couldn't do before. The answer determines the real size of the long-term market. And the real size of that market is much smaller than what investors have priced in.
V. The Consolidation: What Makes This Different
Every previous technology cycle created separate products for separate needs. Word processing. Spreadsheets. Email. Search. Databases. Photo editing. Financial modeling software. Each created its own learning curve, its own vendor relationship, its own subscription, its own lock-in opportunity. The fragmentation wasn't accidental — it was the architecture of extraction, each domain requiring its own tool and therefore its own captive user base.
Large language models — the technical category that includes systems like GPT, Claude, and Gemini, which are trained on vast corpora of text and learn to generate contextually appropriate responses to almost any query — consolidate much of this into a single conversational interface. They don't replace specialized tools entirely, but they eliminate the fragmentation that made users captive to multiple vendors pursuing multiple independent extraction strategies simultaneously.
More importantly: the technology is transparent in a way proprietary software never was. With AI, failure is visible. The system hallucinates — generates plausible-sounding but factually incorrect content — in plain English that the user can read and evaluate. It makes mistakes loudly, in complete sentences, in real time. That visibility is the structural opposite of the silent coercion that defined the software era.
When Amazon buries a product in search results to favor one that paid for placement, it happens invisibly. You cannot see what you're not being shown. The algorithm simply doesn't surface the product that should be there. When an LLM gets something wrong, it does so in complete sentences you can read, question, and verify. That's not a minor distinction. It's the difference between a tool that can be evaluated and one that can only be trusted or abandoned.
This puts informed end users on roughly equivalent footing with the companies deploying the technology. Both are using the same models, the same interfaces, the same underlying capabilities. The information asymmetry that made the software era so extractive — where companies knew things about their products that users couldn't access — has substantially collapsed. You can see what the technology is doing because it does it in the language you already speak.
The technology has also reached an unusual competitive structure. Once the techniques for building capable large language models are established and documented — which is already substantially the case — the core capability is difficult to monopolize. Companies will still attempt to build moats through distribution deals, default settings, proprietary training data, and workflow integration. But the underlying technology resists ownership in a way proprietary software never did. Large language model capability is closer to electricity than to Windows: infrastructure that becomes more valuable when standardized and accessible, not less.
This is precisely why the current market is unsustainable.
VI. The Bubble in Numbers
This is not a claim that the capability disappears. It is a claim that the market's pricing of infinite growth will collide with physical, economic, and structural limits — and that collision is closer than most investors have accounted for.
The scale of capital already deployed makes the intensity of that bet concrete. The four major hyperscalers — Amazon, Alphabet, Meta, and Microsoft — are on track to spend roughly $650 billion in capital expenditures in 2026 alone, much of it directed toward AI infrastructure: data centers, chips, and power-hungry compute clusters. That figure represents a sixty to seventy percent jump from 2025 levels. Private players are matching the frenzy: OpenAI has been valued in the $500 billion range, while Anthropic recently closed a funding round at a reported $380 billion post-money valuation. Nvidia, the dominant supplier of AI accelerators, has seen its market capitalization surge into the trillions on the strength of data center revenue growth. Cumulative hyperscaler AI spending is projected to approach or exceed several trillion dollars by 2030.
Start with Tesla as the most visible example of AI-driven valuation irrationality. As of 2024, Tesla traded at a price-to-earnings ratio exceeding one hundred. At various points in 2023 and 2024, that ratio topped two hundred, briefly approaching one thousand. A price-to-earnings ratio — the relationship between a company's stock price and its annual profit per share — is one of the primary metrics investors use to assess whether a stock is cheap or expensive relative to what the underlying business actually earns. The historical average P/E for the S&P 500 hovers around fifteen to sixteen. Even genuinely high-growth technology companies rarely sustain ratios above fifty for extended periods.
Tesla's valuation assumes not just continued dominance in electric vehicles — a market where competition is intensifying and margins are compressing — but also transformative success in autonomous driving and AI robotics. The company is priced as if speculative future revenues from businesses that don't yet exist at commercial scale are already certain. This is not analysis. It is hope priced as fact.
The pattern extends across the sector. Nvidia — the semiconductor company whose graphics processing chips became the primary hardware for AI training — grew to a market capitalization rivaling Amazon and Google, driven entirely by AI infrastructure demand. The historical precedent is unambiguous. In March 2000, Cisco Systems became the most valuable company in the world by selling the routers and switches — the picks and shovels — for the early internet buildout, trading at a P/E over one hundred. When infrastructure capacity met demand, the stock crashed and took twenty-four years to reclaim its peak. Infrastructure spending follows a characteristic pattern across every technology transition: explosive growth during the initial build-out phase, then sharp decline once capacity meets demand. The semiconductor industry has lived through this cycle repeatedly.
The AI sector's concentration in major stock indices has created systemic risk that extends well beyond AI companies themselves. As of 2024, AI-adjacent companies represented approximately thirty to thirty-five percent of the S&P 500's total market capitalization. That concentration exceeds what technology represented even at the peak of the dot-com bubble in 2000. When valuations correct — not if, but when — the damage will propagate across the entire market.
Then there is the energy problem, which the market has not seriously priced into any major valuation.
A single large training run for a frontier model — the process of teaching a language model by exposing it to vast quantities of text and adjusting its parameters to improve its predictions — can consume fifty or more gigawatt-hours of electricity. One gigawatt-hour is the amount of energy consumed by one million watts of electrical draw for one hour. For context: the average American household uses roughly eleven megawatt-hours per year. A single frontier training run consumes what thousands of households use in an entire year. And these runs happen repeatedly, across multiple models, at multiple competing companies simultaneously.
Training, however, is only the initial expense. Inference — actually running the finished model to respond to user queries — consumes even more power at scale. A data center supporting a major language model at commercial scale can require fifty to one hundred megawatts of continuous electrical draw, with frontier hyperscale facilities increasingly targeting three hundred megawatts to a full gigawatt. That is the equivalent power consumption of a small city to a mid-sized one, running without interruption. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta are each building multiple such facilities. Industry analyses project total annual electricity demand from AI infrastructure reaching eighty to one hundred forty terawatt-hours by 2030 — roughly equivalent to the entire annual electricity consumption of the Netherlands.
This demand is already straining electrical grids in the regions where data centers concentrate. Grid operators in Virginia — which hosts the highest concentration of data centers in the world — Texas, and Ireland have issued formal warnings about capacity constraints. The capital cost of building sufficient renewable energy infrastructure to meet this demand, and the timeline required to permit and construct it, has not appeared in any major company's valuation model in a form commensurate with the actual exposure.
VII. What the Market Is Missing
Here is the fundamental error in current valuations: they assume that current large language model users represent the beginning of exponential mass adoption, rather than the temporary peak of a developmental phase serving a specific and limited population.
Most users don't need large language models. They need the specialized tools that LLM development will eventually make possible. And those tools will require a fraction of the energy, computing power, and infrastructure cost.
Consider the range of knowledge work that AI is expected to transform. A physicist doesn't need access to the full corpus of human written knowledge. She needs a model trained specifically on physics literature, current experimental data, mathematical methods, and relevant adjacent fields. That model might be one-tenth the size of a general-purpose LLM, orders of magnitude cheaper to run per query, and substantially more accurate within its domain because it isn't diluted by training data from contexts where physics doesn't apply.
The same logic applies across disciplines. Medical diagnosis requires a model deeply trained on clinical literature, imaging data, and pharmacological research — not one that can also write poetry and explain Roman history. Legal research requires a model with deep knowledge of case law, statutory interpretation, and jurisdictional variation. Engineering analysis, financial modeling, software debugging — each domain will develop specialized AI tools that outperform general-purpose LLMs on domain-specific tasks while consuming vastly less computational infrastructure.
Domain-specific models are not a distant possibility. They are the predictable next phase of a technology whose general-purpose developmental phase is substantially closer to completion than most investors have acknowledged.
The current generation of large language models is doing essential work: building the techniques, establishing the training methodologies, and generating the synthetic and curated data that will seed the next generation of specialized models. But once that developmental phase completes — and the economic incentives to complete it are overwhelming — the market for general-purpose LLMs contracts dramatically.
The actual long-term user base for utility-scale general-purpose LLMs will be a fraction of current projections. It will consist primarily of people whose work inherently spans multiple sectors and requires access to the full breadth of consolidated knowledge: writers synthesizing across disciplines, strategic analysts working at the intersection of technical and policy domains, researchers operating at disciplinary boundaries where the tools of one field need to speak to the questions of another.
These are people whose value comes from connecting ideas across domains rather than deepening expertise within a single one. This is not a small or unimportant population — it is, in fact, the population that will determine what the specialized models are built to do. But it is not the population that current valuations assume. We are talking about millions of persistent users, not billions. We are talking about utility infrastructure serving a specific professional class, not exponential consumer adoption on the scale of the smartphone.
The internet's maturation offers the clearest historical parallel. Early value accrued disproportionately to infrastructure builders — the backbone providers, the protocols, the pipes. Over time, it migrated upward into applications and services layered atop that foundation. The base layer endured, but it commoditized: essential, yet no longer singular, no longer dominant, and priced accordingly. The companies that thrived were those that understood which layer they actually occupied. The companies that failed were those that believed the infrastructure moment would last forever.
When institutional investors internalize this — when the models that justified current valuations are revised to reflect a user base one-tenth the size — the correction will be severe.
VIII. The Architecture After the Crash, and Why It Won't Be Clean
The rational endpoint of AI market development is a three-tier architecture: utility-scale general-purpose LLMs at the base, specialized domain models in the middle built on top of that infrastructure, and intelligent routing systems at the top that direct queries to the appropriate model based on context. Engineers would design it this way from scratch. The market will arrive at something approximating it, but through a process that is messier, slower, and more destructive of capital than clean design would suggest.
The consolidation will be incomplete. Corporate rivalries, regulatory complexity, and geopolitical fragmentation will preserve inefficiency in the system. Google will not merge its AI infrastructure with Microsoft's even when economics would recommend it. China will maintain its own parallel LLM infrastructure regardless of redundancy with Western systems. The European Union may require localized infrastructure for data sovereignty reasons that are legitimate in principle but economically suboptimal in practice. Some redundancy is the floor, not the ceiling.
The crash will also destroy capital that specialized AI development actually requires. The companies best positioned to build domain-specific models are often startups with deep expertise in specific fields but limited access to computational resources and infrastructure. These companies depend on the capital markets that the bubble is currently inflating. When the correction comes, funding will evaporate precisely as the specialized model opportunity becomes most viable. Some of the most useful AI development will stall for years while markets recover and new investment theses take shape.
The survivors will be companies with diversified revenue streams, not pure-play AI firms. Microsoft survives because it has Office, Azure, and enterprise software relationships that predate AI. Google survives because it has search advertising and cloud infrastructure. Amazon survives because of AWS and its retail extraction machine. Pure AI companies — those whose entire valuation rests on LLM infrastructure — face existential risk. The path to profitability for companies like OpenAI requires continuous growth in paid subscriptions and enterprise adoption at scales that the actual user base will not support.
Government intervention will arrive eventually, and it will be clumsy. When AI infrastructure is eventually declared strategically critical and moves toward utility regulation — the treatment historically applied to infrastructure like telecommunications and electrical grids, where private ownership requires public oversight of pricing and access — the regulatory process will favor incumbents and create new extraction opportunities disguised as public-private partnerships.
This is not speculation. It is the documented pattern of every infrastructure transition from private to regulated utility in American economic history. The Telecommunications Act of 1996, for example, was pitched as a mechanism to open local telephone markets to competition; instead, it catalyzed massive consolidation, allowing the incumbent Baby Bells to re-merge into the regional oligopolies that dominate today. Energy market restructuring, broadband infrastructure policy — each transition produced regulatory frameworks that protected the most connected incumbents and locked in suboptimal technical choices for decades.
The messy result: a partially consolidated infrastructure that is more efficient than today's redundant system but less efficient than rational design would produce. Some companies collapse. Others limp along as regulated utilities extracting modest rents from guaranteed customer bases. A few — those positioned at the middleware layer, building specialized domain tools on top of consolidated general infrastructure — will find that the post-crash landscape suits them precisely.
IX. The Agent Trap
The major technology companies see the consolidation dynamic coming. Their response is already taking shape in the form of AI agents — systems designed to act on a user's behalf rather than wait for explicit prompts. Book your travel. Manage your calendar. Handle your email. Draft and send routine correspondence. The proposition is that users set high-level goals and the agent executes the details.
The agent model requires the one resource that twenty years of extractive technology has thoroughly strip-mined: unconditional trust. After antivirus extortion, hidden fee structures, search algorithms optimized to serve advertisers over users, and social media platforms engineered to maximize engagement at the cost of user wellbeing, the same companies are now asking for calendar access, bank authorization, and autonomous action in the user's name. They are selling a butler. We remember that they're landlords.
The agent model also requires opacity — the ability to act without the user reviewing every action — which directly contradicts the transparency that makes current LLMs evaluable. Companies will attempt to build new moats through proprietary fine-tuning, ecosystem lock-in, and vertical integration. Whether they succeed depends on whether they can rebuild trust that the extractive era systematically destroyed. The difficulty of that problem is, for once, the user's structural advantage.
X. What Comes After
When the correction comes — when redundant infrastructure is forced toward consolidation, when business models are rationalized to match actual rather than projected user bases, when the energy constraint becomes impossible to ignore — what remains will be closer to utility than product.
Utility, in the economic sense, means infrastructure that is regulated, standardized, and priced for access rather than for extraction. Electrical utilities don't charge you more because you've become dependent on electricity. They charge the regulated rate for the kilowatt-hours you consume, with oversight structures designed to prevent monopoly pricing. Telephone utilities, before the advent of mobile competition, operated under similar constraints. The utility model is not ideal — regulated utilities are often inefficient, slow to innovate, and captured by the regulatory agencies that are supposed to oversee them — but it is structurally incompatible with the darkest patterns of the extractive software era.
LLMs will become infrastructure of this type, not because companies will choose it but because the economics won't support the alternative. The technology is too transparent to sustain manufactured complexity. It is too dependent on user trust to function once trust is withdrawn. It is too difficult to monopolize once the underlying techniques are established and documented. The extractive phase ends not because corporate ethics improve but because the technology itself doesn't permit the same mechanisms that made the software era profitable for vendors and miserable for users.
Not immediately. Not cleanly. The transition will be ugly, capital will be destroyed, and the regulatory frameworks that emerge will be imperfect. But the direction is determinable, even if the timeline is not.
I've been the user at the terminal since 1983. I watched technology move from institutional research to consumer product, from genuine tool to extraction mechanism, and — possibly, for the first time — back toward tool again. I watched the antivirus scam play out in real time. I watched Amazon spend twenty years building a trap. I've had the experience of being on the wrong end of every dark pattern the software era invented.
And two years ago I found a technology that, for the first time since that VAX terminal, did what I asked it to do without asking for anything in return that I hadn't agreed to. That experience — the specific, lived experience of someone the software era had shut out of written communication for four decades — is what positions me to see the market analysis clearly. The bubble will correct, probably within five years. The consolidation will be messier than rational design would suggest. But what emerges after will determine whether technology finally serves the people who use it, or whether we enter another cycle of extraction wearing a friendlier face.
The case for the former rests not on optimism about corporate behavior — the record on that is unambiguous — but on the structural properties of the technology itself. You cannot hide what an LLM is doing. You cannot manufacture complexity to justify ongoing subscription fees when the product either works or it doesn't, and users can verify that directly, in their own language, in real time.
What remains, after the correction and the consolidation, might finally be what I've been waiting for since 1983. Not a god-mind or a digital butler, but a cursor that blinks, waits for input, and helps do the work without intercepting it. The VAX terminal is gone. The promise of it — technology that amplifies human intent without inserting itself between the intent and the outcome — may finally be arriving. The bubble will burst. The consolidation will be ugly. But on the other side of it is infrastructure, and infrastructure, at its best, is just the thing that makes the work possible.
